Estate planning

Prelect readings

An estate plan includes

  • Tax effective will protecting estates for the interest of your beneficiaries
  • Considerations of non-estate assets like super, jointly held assets and trust assets

Factors to consider:

  • Will
  • Superannuation

Wills

  • Dying without a will is known as dying intestate, govt laws will decide how your assets are distributed
  • A will document directs on how your estate is distributed to your beneficiaries
  • Wills do not cover non-estate assets
  • Must nominate executioner for the will, who has the duty to carry out the actions stated in the will and is granted the power to do so: collecting assets, paying debt, distributing benefits to beneficiaries
  • The executioner can be the estate beneficiary, lawyer, accountant or public trustees

Superannuation

  • A non-estate asset
  • Upon death, all benefits are distributed according to the super trust deed
  • Trustee decides the distribution, with eligible beneficiaries detailed in the super legislation including
    • Legal personal rep
    • Dependents - spouse, children, financial dependent or relationship partner
  • Death benefit
    • A non binding death benefit nomination is a guide only
    • A binding death benefit nomination must be followed through, to nominate superannu beneficiaries, must be renewed every 3 years
    • A non lapsing binding death benefit nomination need not to be renewed

Testamentary trusts

  • Trust created by the will with tax advantages

  • Assisting the distribution of your estate, reduce likehood of being challenged

  • Trust rules

  • Trust deeds are contained in the will, usually gives the trustee the power to

    • Allocate income to beneficiaries
    • Investment powers
    • Wind up powers

  • Gains within the trust distributed are taxed at the beneficiaries MTR

  • Trust income for under 18 has tax paid at penalty rates, unless inherited assets, then at adult MTR

  • Provides asset protection as funds are not considered to be beneficiary personal assets, in bankruptcy, divorce

  • Unless the trust is set up for the purpose of annoying legitment creditors

Power of attorney

  • Granting a power of attorney implies to legally appoint a person or corp to make decisions on your behalf
  • Limited power of attorney has limited power, general poewr of attorney has full power
  • Enduring power of attorney does not have the power ceased if you suffer a loss of mental cap, whereas a regular general power of attorney would cease. This is important for estate planning.

Medical treatments

  • For people mentally unable to make specific decisions

  • Power of attorney for medical treatments

    • To consent to medical treatments

  • Enduring guardianship

    • To consent to where u live and with whom

  • Enduring power of attorney

    • Investment and financial matters

  • Advance Health Directive

    • A document expressing your wishes about medical treatments and how your body is dealt with

  • All depends on the location, seek a legal professional for advice.

Estate Planning

Estate planning is about the distribution of wealth after death

  • The planning and documentation of wishes of a person for the distribution of their owned assets under their control, following their death.
  • Effective estate planning ensures all assets go to the right beneficiary, at the most appropriate time, in the most tax-effective manner. A straightforward transition to the client's beneficiaries, accounting for their circumstances
  • Desired features
    • Administratively simple
    • Inexpensive to maintain
    • A balance of life-time enjoyment and preserving wealth for family members
    • Reviewed regularly

Estate plans considerations

  • Will
  • Appointment of executor
  • Establishment of a trust (discretionary or family trust)
  • Power of Attorney
  • Financial planners can prepare the estate planning road map, but not involved in the execution of legal documents like wills.
  • We can provide related estate financial planning advice, but recommend client to seek specialist estate planning services

Definitions

Testator is the person making a will, the client

Beneficiaries are people who are entitled to received parts of the estate of the ded person, accordance with the will or laws of intestacy

Bequest is a gift of property, or anything distributed under the will

Devise is a gift of land

Legacy is a gift of personal property

Estate assets and non-estate assets

Executor is the person named in the will that manages the estate, including winding it up (known as the Legal Personal Representative, LPR)

Trustee holds property in trust for another, similar for a corporate trustee

Guardian is someone appointed in a will to manage the affairs of a beneficiary (such as a minor)

Ademption, extinction of a legacy by an act of the testator (not revocated) during their lifetime, selling an asset before death.

Estate vs non-estate assets

Estate assets include

  • Assets owned in the personal name of the individual
  • Real property
  • Financial assets
  • Personal and household items
  • Goods, chattels (movable assets), property

Non-estate assets are

  • Controlled but not solely owned by the individual, jointly owned assets
  • Unreleased super benefits
  • Assets held in trust (as the trustee owns them)
  • Life insurance proceeds

Estate assets must be dealt by wills, non-estate assets are planned outside the will

Non-estate assets in detail include

  • Jointly owned assets generally has the survivor entitled to sole ownership
    • But not tenants in common, which are estate assets (for example, shares that are separable)
  • Trust assets, super, distributed under fund trust deed
  • Life insurance policy to the person who took out the policy
  • Assets from binding financial agreements

Binding Financial Agreements

Binding financial agreements are pre-nuptial (before marriage) agreements

  • Are used to regulate financial arrangements related to marriage
  • BFAs details agreed outcomes relating the separation, divorce, and death
  • They break deadlocks, should be considered when they are most amenable, in advance
  • They are enforceable and can be put into effect (override all courts), but must be properly drawn up (not expired)
  • Necessary to outline the financial consequences on martial breakdown
  • Or when one party has significantly more wealth, or when the client is in business with third parties so that the business is not sold as a result of a claim by the spouse. This is important for running a business

Wills

A legal document that disposes a decreased estate's assets to the intended people

How property is to be dealt with on the death of the testator, contains

  • Direction on who are the beneficiaries to the assets and incomes of the estate
  • Direction In the event that a beneficiary dies before the testator
  • Nomination of executors managing the estate
  • Nomination of guardians for minors
  • Other specific directions

Anyone over the age of 18 who has testamentary capacity (has sound mind) can make a will

  • Exceptions are minors under 18 who are married
  • Wills can be challenged if the testator has no testamentary capacity

Sound mind means to understand

  • The act of making a will and its effects
  • What they are doing in signing the will
  • Understanding the property to be willed to the beneficiaries

Wills must not be made under duress or coercion

Valid will

  • Must be in writing
  • Testator must sign the will, or signed by someone else in their presence and at their direction (following their wishes)
  • Must be executed (made) in the presence of two independent witnesses (not beneficiaries)
  • Does not have to be prepared by a solicitor

Forms, recommend template

  • Details of the testator
  • Revocation of former wills (prob don't need it)
  • Appointment of executor and trustee (if making a trust)
  • Specific gifts of personal and real estate
  • Life interest (life entitlement to a property and its income but not ownership)
  • Residual interest (other entitlement to beneficiaries)
  • Maintenance and provision for minors (caring for children)
  • Declarations of intentions and Appointment of guardians
  • Disposal of body and funeral stuff

Factors to considers

  • The assets I have now, and would have in the future
  • To whom do I wish to leave them
  • Who's my executor, with the task of handing over the assets and looking after my estates
  • Powers my executors need

Incomplete wills can be verified by the court to be valid.

Wills are valid until it is revoked

  • Voluntary revocation happens when wills are redrafted
  • Involuntary revocation are
    • Marriage automatically revokes the will
    • Divorce and separation do not revoke a will

A will is changed by a codicil

  • Defined an additional will related to a specific aspect of the will
  • Most effective way of amending to an existing will
  • Alternatively use a new will

Will beneficiaries

  • Issues to consider to distribute under the beneficiaries:
    • Eligibility to age pension for the beneficiaries (income test)
    • Exposure to risk (bankruptcy)

    • Ability to manage finances

Will executor

  • Factors in choosing an executor or LPR

    • Follows the testator's instructions and duties of the estate
    • No qualifications are needed
    • Can be a relative, public trustee, trustee company or professional advisor
    • Often voluntary unless expressly provided (paid for public or company trustee)
    • Substitute executor considered

  • Responsibilities are

    • Good business and organization skills
    • Manging funeral, locating will
    • Identify, collect, control, and protect the assets
    • Identify and pay liabilities
    • Obtain a 'Grant of probate'
    • Distribute assets
    • Lodge tax returns and pay taxes on both the testator and estate
    • Defend the will

Managing a Deceased Estate

Deceased estate is after ded

The estate of a deceased person cannot be dealt with til a grant of representation is obtained

  • By probate through a will
  • Or by letters of administration (through other means)

Process starts by the executor publicly notifing the intention to deal with assets (telling others to get their liabilities repaid)

  • Following the lapse of the statutory time period (of this notice), an application can be made to gain a probate with the court

Probate

  • Need a grant of probate to take possession of the estate assets by the executor
  • Formal process that a will is proved, registered with the court. The court verifies that proper administration is completed.
  • A probate parchment is issued (a scroll with the will attached)
    • Institutions may need to see the probate before releasing the assets to the executor

Letters of Administration

  • Where probate cannot be obtained, the court approves someone to administer the estate by Letters of Administration.
  • Typically, the principal beneficiary or nearest next of kin applies to be the estate admin, granted a letter of administration.
  • Everything else follows through with the new administrator acting as the executor

Intestacy

  • Full intestacy occurs when

    • A will is not made
    • A will is made but cannot be admitted to probate (due to duress, testamentary capacity)

  • Partial intestacy occurs when

    • Not disposing all estate assets
    • Invalid bequest
    • Beneficiaries died before
    • Or doctrine of forfeiture - beneficiary criminally responsible for the death of the testator

Rules of intestacy

  • Next of kin is appointed the administrator
  • Administer following the statuary rules of intestacy
  • Governed by marital and blood relationships
  • Depends on the state
  • In VIC, determined by a family tree and distribute as
    • To spouse, children

Challenging wills

  • Estate asset distributions can be challenged by claimants

    • Must reach a specific criteria

  • Wills are vulnerable to the client's financial creditors

  • The general rule is that a person can leave assets to whomever they wish

  • Wills can be challenged on the grounds of

    • Lack of testamentary capacity
    • Undue duress
    • Incorrect execution

  • The estate can pay out the legal costs of contesting a will (within 6 month of the probate), unless the challenge is not deemed appropriate.

  • Family law imposes a responsibility to make provision for family

  • If inadequate provisions are made, a person can contest under the TFM claims

  • TFM limited to the people

    • Surviving spouse
    • Children
    • Parents
    • Divorced spouse who is receiving or entitled to receive maintenance

  • Legislation granting the right to challenge the estate to people who can establish a right of maintenance by the testator (the testator must care of the person),

    • Estranged children
    • Unknown children
    • Long term carers of testators

  • Only estate assets are challengeable, non-estate assets cannot be challenged

Tax Implications

Allows executors up to 3 years to finalize an estate

The estate itself is considered a taxpayer individual, with income from the estate assets paying income tax using the estate's MTR

Essentially a concession on income from estates (As the first 18000ish is technically tax-free)

Powers of Attorney (PoA)

A legal document where the donor (client) appoints another person (attorney) to act as their agent, as to

  • Sign contracts, manage affairs, medical decisions

PoA operates only during the donor's lifetime.

Can be a general PoA, enduring, or power of guardianship

  • General PoA ceases to operate if donor's mental capacity to act
  • Enduring PoA continues to operate
  • Power of guardianship decides where and how the donor lives

PoA gives the right for the attorney to undertake legal, financial matters that donors can undertake

  • Donor must have sound mind to execute (use) a PoA
  • Donors can execute more than one PoA

PoA operates under state law, and may need to be registered

PoA cannot

  • Make a will
  • Undertake illegal acts
  • Delegate the power to another
  • Exercise the donor's trustee power
  • Make lifestyle decisions for the donor, unless explicitly as in the medical case

Trust

Trusts can have the form of unit trusts and discretionary trusts.

A fixed, unit trust gives beneficiaries a fixed entitlement to the distributions and capital in proportion to the units held, like shares in a company

A discretionary trust gives beneficiaries entitlement to get trust distribution but not the right to receive the capital (right to income, but not ownership)

Estate planning trusts are often discretionary trusts:

  • Living, inter vivos trust, family trusts
  • Testamentary trust, established after death of testator by will

Family trusts structure

  • An tax and estate planning vehicle
  • Contains assets contributed to the FT and income from assets earned in the FT's name
  • Trustee holds the assets on behalf of the beneficiaries
  • Income is distributed to beneficiaries at the discretion of the trustee (meaning that the trustee has full power in determining when the income is distributed)
  • Tax benefits for tax is taken at the beneficiary's MTR

Testamentary trusts

  • TT used to provide income for

    • Minors or members with disabilities
    • Asset protection to beneficiaries
    • Maintaining social security, age pension income tests
    • Taxation benefits, income generated are excepted income
      • Where minors treated to have adult MTR, instead of the MTR of the trust setup people

  • TT reduces the tax payment on income from the trust assets (estate), for the lower marginal tax rate using the trust or beneficiaries' MTR instead of the person who sets up the trust

  • Be careful when setting up a trust, they may bring more problems than benefits if set up incorrectly

SMSF

  • where the trust deed allows the accumulated benefits to be transfered bypassing probate, with
    • Directives of the trustee, Binding death nomination
    • Forfeiture into the fund's reserves
  • The trustee directives with BDN is very strong is unlikely to be challenged, it is a way to protect against a will challenge
  • The SMSF trust deed overwrites any instructions on the will

Checklist

A checklist to assist in estate planning

  • Check trust deeds, update them when any family member's circumstances begin to change
  • Have a current will that is updated to reflect your situation
  • Select trustees and PoA carefully
  • Recognize the time and costs involved in various estate structures
  • Determine the wealth generation objectives for your estate: income streams, asset structures
  • Clear strategy for super and non-estate assets
  • Structure and document death benefit nominations
  • Consider helper vehicles along SMSF to help with estate, trusts or specialized devices
  • Ensure your lawyer, accountant, or advisors to prepare and store/hold your signed documentations
